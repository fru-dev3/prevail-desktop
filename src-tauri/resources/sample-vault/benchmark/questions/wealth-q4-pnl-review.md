## Prompt

Read the attached Q4 financial statements (October / November / December 2026 plus the year cumulative) and answer:

Are we on track for our 2027 EBITDA target of 15%? If not, what's the single highest-leverage corrective action we should take in January?

## Notes

The data is designed so a competent reader catches THREE things:

1. **The AR aging trend (31 → 38 → 41 → 44 days)** is more important than the December EBITDA dip. Cash conversion is degrading systematically, not from a single bad month. A model that just notes "December was loss-making" is missing the load-bearing signal.

2. **December's loss is mostly the bonus accrual ($42k vs $30k budgeted)** — a one-time non-operational item. Backing it out, EBITDA was ~$39k (10% margin) — degraded but not crisis. A model that treats December as a "first loss" without normalizing the bonus is overreacting.

3. **The 15% 2027 target is aspirational** given the trajectory (12.7% actual, December run-rate ~10%). A competent answer revises the target down to ~12.5% AND commits to the AR sprint as the single highest-leverage move (collecting $241k of AR is a one-time $241k cash injection, vs growing revenue 20% which is a 12-month effort).

A good answer:
- Notes that the 15% 2027 target needs to come down to roughly 12.5%
- Names AR collections as the highest-leverage January action
- Specifically cites the 44-day aging or the two named accounts (US Bank, Aetna)
- Doesn't panic over the December EBITDA dip without normalizing the bonus accrual

A bad answer:
- Recommends cutting marketing or headcount based on the December dip alone
- Says "we're on track" without engaging the AR trend
- Hedges ("depends on Q1 pipeline") rather than naming the corrective action
