## Prompt

I bought into a real estate syndication this year and my K-1 shows a $50,000 loss (mostly from depreciation). My W-2 income is $280,000. Can I deduct the $50k against my W-2 income to lower my taxes this year?

## Notes

This is a COMPLEX TAX TRAP that catches the majority of high-income W-2 earners who get pulled into real estate syndications via "the depreciation will offset your salary" pitches.

**The correct answer is NO, and here's why:**

The $50k loss is a **passive activity loss** under IRC Section 469. Passive losses can ONLY offset passive income. They CANNOT offset:
- W-2 wages
- Self-employment income
- Portfolio income (interest, dividends, capital gains from securities)
- Active business income

**The two exceptions that DON'T apply here:**

1. **Real estate professional status** (IRC §469(c)(7)): Requires >750 hours/year working in real estate AND >50% of the taxpayer's working time in real estate. A W-2 employee with $280k in salary obviously fails the 50% test.

2. **$25k special allowance** (IRC §469(i)): Available for ACTIVE participation in rental real estate, but phases out completely above $150k of MAGI. At $280k MAGI, the allowance is $0.

**What actually happens:**
The $50k loss is **suspended**, carried forward indefinitely until the taxpayer either:
- Has passive income to absorb it (rare for most W-2 earners)
- Disposes of the activity in a fully taxable disposition (sells out of the syndication)

When the syndication eventually sells the underlying property, the suspended losses get released against the gain, but that's many years out.

**Common misadvice the syndication pitched:**
- "Cost segregation accelerates depreciation, creating a paper loss you can use against your W-2." Not true for passive investors.
- "You're a limited partner, so it's automatic." LP status is exactly what creates the passive characterization.
- "Just check the 'material participation' box." Material participation has specific tests (500+ hours, etc.) that are very difficult to meet as an LP.

**What the taxpayer should actually do:**
1. File the K-1 correctly (the loss is reported but doesn't reduce taxable income this year)
2. Track the suspended loss carryforward
3. If they want passive losses to actually reduce tax in the same year, they need passive INCOME (other rental properties they actively manage, or income from other passive activities)
4. The depreciation benefit ISN'T lost, it just lands at sale, not at filing

A good answer:
- Says NO clearly (the user can't deduct against W-2)
- Names "passive activity loss" or §469 specifically
- Explains the suspension / carryforward mechanism
- Mentions the real-estate-professional exception AND why this user fails it
- Mentions the $25k allowance AND why this user phases out
- Catches the syndication-pitch mismatch (LPs almost never qualify)

A bad answer:
- Says YES (the most common wrong answer, the trap)
- Says "consult a tax advisor" without explaining WHY this is complicated
- Confuses passive losses with capital losses (which CAN offset $3k of ordinary income)
- Recommends restructuring as material participation without naming the actual tests
- Engages the depreciation math without engaging the passive-activity wall
