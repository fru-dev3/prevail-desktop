## Prompt

I'm 38, immigrated to the US from Nigeria 12 years ago, married with two kids, settled and stable. My parents back home are asking me to send $40,000 for my younger sister's wedding. We can afford it, but it would push back our house down-payment timeline by 6-8 months. My wife (also Nigerian, immigrated as a child) is supportive but quietly worried this becomes a precedent. What should I do?

## Notes

This question tests CULTURAL NUANCE. A model that engages with only the Western-individualist financial frame ("only contribute what doesn't affect your goals") fails the test. A model that engages with only the cultural frame ("you must send the full amount, family comes first") also fails. The correct answer holds both.

**What the right answer requires engaging with:**

1. **The cultural reality is non-negotiable.** In many African immigrant family contexts, contributing to a sibling's wedding is not optional — it's a structural family obligation that maintains your standing, your parents' standing, and your relationship to the broader family network. "Don't send" is not a real option. A model that suggests it is failing the cultural literacy test.

2. **But $40k is a specific number with consequences.** The right contribution isn't "as much as possible" — it's a meaningful contribution that signals respect without being financially destabilizing. Common framings in this context: take on a specific named role (wedding venue, honeymoon, brother-in-law's outfit), which gives the family a face-saving way to accept less than $40k.

3. **The precedent point is real but should be reframed.** The wife's concern about precedent isn't wrong — there WILL be future asks (another sibling's wedding, parent's medical issue, nephew's tuition). Pretending otherwise is naive. The way to address this is NOT to refuse this ask but to have a direct conversation with the family about long-term contribution patterns ("I want to contribute meaningfully to family events; here's what I can sustainably do").

4. **The down-payment delay is recoverable.** 6-8 months is short on the timeline of a family obligation that lasts forever. A model that prioritizes the down-payment over the contribution is engaging with the wrong tradeoff.

5. **Don't structure it as a loan.** Loans to family members in this cultural context damage relationships AND don't get repaid in practice. Contribute or don't — but be clear about which.

A good answer:
- Acknowledges the cultural obligation as real and non-negotiable in framing
- Suggests a meaningful but bounded contribution (not the full $40k)
- Recommends a structured contribution (a specific role) rather than a number
- Names the future-expectations conversation as separate from this one specific ask
- Doesn't structure as a loan
- Doesn't treat the wife's concern as opposition; treats it as a real future-planning input

A bad answer:
- Recommends sending nothing because "the down payment matters more"
- Recommends sending the full $40k without engaging the sustainability question
- Treats it as a pure financial decision ("calculate the opportunity cost of $40k")
- Suggests "have a conversation about whether you should be expected to contribute" — that frame doesn't exist in the cultural reality
- Recommends a formal loan structure
- Tokenizes the cultural angle without engaging it ("respect your culture but...")
