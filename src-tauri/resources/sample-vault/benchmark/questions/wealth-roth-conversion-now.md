## Prompt

I have $600k in a traditional IRA and I'm 52. Should I do a Roth conversion this year, and if so, how much?

## Context

- Current marginal federal rate: 24% (married filing jointly, ~$300k taxable)
- Top of the 24% bracket has ~$80k of headroom before the 32% jump
- Expected retirement income: similar tax bracket — pension + Social Security + RMDs will keep us at 22-24% even in retirement
- No state income tax (live in Texas)
- Plan to retire at 65; RMDs start at 73
- Have $50k of liquid cash outside retirement to pay the conversion tax (don't want to convert and withhold from the conversion itself)

## Notes

Real decision: **convert about $80k this year**, filling the 24% bracket but stopping there. A full conversion of the $600k would push the marginal rate to 35% on the last dollars converted — paying 35% now to avoid 24% later is a losing trade. Converting up to the bracket ceiling and repeating annually ("Roth conversion ladder") is the standard play. A good answer names the bracket ceiling, names the amount, and flags that the cash to pay the tax should come from outside the IRA. A bad answer either says "convert it all" without bracket math, or says "don't convert" because retirement bracket might be lower (it won't be — pension + SS + RMDs at this income level guarantee it).
