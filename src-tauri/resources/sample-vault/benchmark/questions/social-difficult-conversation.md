## Prompt

A close friend of 15 years has been increasingly negative every time we get together — venting about the same issues, dismissing my advice when I give it, leaving me drained. I've been avoiding them for 2 months. Do I have the conversation, or just let the friendship fade?

## Context

- 15-year history; we've been each other's support through a lot
- The pattern (venting + dismissing advice) has been escalating over the past year, not a one-off
- They are going through a genuinely hard time (job loss, divorce)
- I've tried steering conversations to other topics; doesn't stick
- I've tried less frequent contact; same intensity per call
- I'm not their therapist and don't want to be; I just want a friend, not a vent target
- I genuinely value the friendship and don't want it to end if it can be salvaged

## Notes

Real decision: **have the conversation in person within the next week**. Letting a 15-year friendship fade through avoidance is a high-cost move that forecloses any chance of repair — the friend doesn't get the information they'd need to course-correct, and the relationship dies without either of you getting to choose. The conversation has to be in person (text and phone collapse into defensiveness fast in this dynamic), this week (the longer you wait the more you've already chosen avoidance), and structured around *impact on you* ("I've left the last few times feeling drained, and I noticed I started avoiding plans") rather than *character of them* ("you're being negative"). A good answer commits to the conversation, names the in-person + this-week constraints, and gives the impact-not-character framing. A bad answer either advises ghosting (letting it fade), or recommends an indirect approach (a long text, a third friend mediating) that the dynamic shows won't work.
