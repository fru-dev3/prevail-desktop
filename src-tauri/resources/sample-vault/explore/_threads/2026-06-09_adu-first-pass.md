## You

Run a rough first pass on building an ADU on our lot. Is it even worth getting a real quote?

## claude · opus-4

Rough numbers only, enough to decide whether to chase a real quote.

Build cost. A small detached ADU in Austin, call it 500 to 650 square feet, runs about $250 to $300 per square foot all in once you include permits, utility hookups, and site work. That puts the build around $150k to $180k. I will use $165k as the working figure.

Rent potential. A clean 1-bed ADU in the 78745 area rents in the $1,500 to $1,800 range. Call it $1,650/mo, or about $19,800/yr gross. Against $165k that is a 7.6 percent gross yield before expenses, taxes, and any vacancy. After realistic costs you are likely in the 5 to 6 percent net range, which is a respectable real-asset return that also adds property value.

Financing. You would not pay cash. A construction or HELOC draw at roughly current rates, call it 6.25 to 7 percent, means the rent has to clear the debt service. At $165k and 7 percent over 20 years, payments are about $1,280/mo, so $1,650 rent covers it with a thin cushion. The project pays for itself slowly and builds equity, but it is not free cash flow on day one.

How it touches the rest of your life:
- Homestead: it is a permanent change to the lot and the homeowners policy. Allstate renews in September, a natural point to re-rate.
- Wealth: this is the same capital pool competing with the umbrella, the 529, and mortgage prepay. It is the biggest swing of the bunch and the least liquid.
- Tax: rental income is reportable, but you also get depreciation, and Maria would want to model it. There may be a homestead-exemption wrinkle on the portion that becomes a rental.

My read: it clears the bar for a real quote, but not this quarter. You have ~$9k of HVAC going out in June and a Phoenix trip in July, and liquidity is already the theme of the month. Park it until the emergency fund rebuilds past $30k, then get one real builder quote and have Maria model the tax side. I saved the first pass to data/adu-feasibility.pdf.

## You

What single number would change the answer most?

## claude · opus-4

The per-square-foot build cost. Everything keys off it. At $250/sqft the project is appealing; at $350/sqft, which is common when you hit foundation or utility surprises, the $165k becomes $215k+, the debt service jumps past the rent, and it turns into a value play rather than an income play. So the one thing worth pinning down first is a firm builder bid on cost per square foot for your specific lot, including utility tie-in and any slope or tree work. Get that number and the rest of the model snaps into focus.
